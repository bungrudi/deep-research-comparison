The Neuro-Metabolic Frontier: A Critical Evaluation of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists in Alzheimer’s Disease and Substance Use Disorders (2023–2026)The landscape of metabolic medicine has undergone a seismic shift between 2023 and 2026, driven by the explosive adoption of glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) such as semaglutide and tirzepatide. While their primary clinical utility remains rooted in the management of type 2 diabetes and obesity, a parallel scientific narrative has emerged, positioning these incretin mimetics as potential multi-system therapeutics for neurodegenerative and neuropsychiatric conditions. This comprehensive analysis evaluates the clinical trial data for two high-stakes non-weight-loss indications: Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and alcohol/substance use disorders (SUD). By contrasting peer-reviewed efficacy data and adverse event profiles against the prevailing mainstream media narratives and corporate pipeline projections, this report identifies the critical junctures where clinical evidence remains robust, where it is preliminary, and where public and investor expectations have decoupled from the scientific reality.The Alzheimer’s Disease Conundrum: From Prophylaxis to InterventionThe investigation into GLP-1RAs as a treatment for Alzheimer’s disease was originally sparked by robust observational and preclinical data. Epidemiological studies consistently identified a lower incidence of dementia among patients with type 2 diabetes who were treated with GLP-1RAs compared to those on other glucose-lowering agents, such as insulins or DPP-4 inhibitors. These observations were bolstered by preclinical models demonstrating that GLP-1 receptor activation could mitigate neuroinflammation, improve cerebral glucose metabolism, and reduce the accumulation of amyloid-$\beta$ and tau proteins—the hallmarks of AD pathology. However, the transition from observational prophylaxis to interventional efficacy in symptomatic populations has proven fraught with complexity.Analysis of the Phase 3 EVOKE and EVOKE+ Clinical TrialsThe most anticipated data in the neuro-metabolic space arrived in late 2025 with the topline readout of Novo Nordisk’s Phase 3 EVOKE (NCT04777396) and EVOKE+ (NCT04777409) trials. These studies represented the largest clinical evaluation of a GLP-1RA in Alzheimer’s disease to date, enrolling a combined 3,808 participants across a global network of research sites. The trials targeted patients with early symptomatic Alzheimer’s disease, defined by mild cognitive impairment (MCI) or mild dementia, with amyloid pathology confirmed via positron emission tomography (PET) or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis.The primary objective of both trials was to determine whether oral semaglutide, administered at a daily dose of 14 mg, could slow the rate of clinical progression compared to placebo over a 104-week treatment period. The decision was based on a calculated risk-benefit analysis, which determined that further treatment was unlikely to yield a clinical signal that would meet regulatory thresholds for approval.The Phase 2b ELAD Trial: A Contrasting PerspectiveThe disappointing results of the EVOKE program stand in contrast to the more optimistic, yet nuanced, findings of the ELAD (Evaluating Liraglutide in Alzheimer's Disease) study, a Phase 2b academic trial led by researchers at Imperial College London. This study randomized 204 non-diabetic patients with mild to moderate Alzheimer’s disease to receive daily subcutaneous injections of liraglutide or a placebo for 52 weeks.The ELAD trial failed its primary endpoint, which was a change in the cerebral glucose metabolic rate in the cortical regions as measured by FDG-PET scans (difference = $-0.17$; $95\% CI$: $-0.39$ to $0.06$; $p = 0.14$). However, several predefined secondary outcomes were highly favorable. MRI analysis revealed that patients treated with liraglutide experienced nearly $50\%$ less brain volume loss across the temporal lobe and total grey matter compared to the placebo arm. Furthermore, cognitive testing showed an $18\%$ slower decline in executive function and memory over the 12-month period.The divergence between the ELAD and EVOKE results highlights critical variables in incremental pharmacology for neurodegeneration. Liraglutide, despite being an older molecule, was administered subcutaneously, which may offer different pharmacokinetic profiles compared to the oral formulation of semaglutide used in EVOKE. Additionally, the trial populations differed; EVOKE targeted an earlier symptomatic stage (MCI/mild dementia) with confirmed amyloid pathology, whereas ELAD included a broader "mild to moderate" clinical diagnosis. Some experts suggest that the neuroprotective effects of GLP-1RAs may be more evident in preserving brain volume rather than reversing the cognitive deficits that have already manifested.Evolving Hypotheses and the Blood-Brain Barrier ChallengeA fundamental limitation cited by neuroscientists is the restricted ability of GLP-1RAs to penetrate the blood-brain barrier (BBB). While GLP-1 receptors are expressed in the hippocampus and other cognitive hubs, these large peptide molecules primarily enter the brain through circumventricular organs or exert effects through indirect signaling via the vagus nerve and the gut-brain axis.Researchers have argued that the failure of semaglutide monotherapy in Phase 3 underscores that Alzheimer’s disease is a multifactorial condition that cannot be shifted by targeting a single metabolic pathway once significant neurodegeneration has occurred. This has shifted the scientific focus toward combination strategies. With the FDA approvals of anti-amyloid antibodies like lecanemab (Leqembi) and donanemab (Kisunla) in 2023 and 2024, the industry is now exploring whether GLP-1RAs can serve as an adjunctive treatment to improve metabolic resilience in neurons while the antibodies clear the toxic protein aggregates.Addiction and Substance Use Disorders: The Anti-Craving SignalIn parallel with the setbacks in neurodegeneration, the use of GLP-1RAs for the treatment of substance use disorders (SUD) has emerged as one of the most promising avenues of research between 2023 and 2026. This trajectory was initiated by anecdotal reports from patients taking semaglutide for weight loss, who described a sudden and profound loss of interest in alcohol, nicotine, and recreational drugs. This investigator-initiated Phase 2 trial evaluated the effects of subcutaneous semaglutide on alcohol consumption and craving in 48 non-treatment-seeking adults with moderate to severe AUD. GLP-1 receptors are expressed in the nucleus accumbens (NAc) and the ventral tegmental area (VTA), regions that mediate the release of dopamine in response to pleasurable stimuli. Preclinical and early human neuroimaging data suggest that GLP-1RAs "mute" the dopaminergic response to addictive drugs. By attenuating the reinforcing properties of alcohol, nicotine, and opioids, these medications allow the individual to exert more rational control over their impulses. This effect appears to be a direct consequence of receptor activation in reward-related regions, which may explain why the benefits are observed across disparate classes of addictive substances.Comparative Safety Profiles: Neurodegenerative vs. Psychiatric PopulationsThe safety and tolerability of GLP-1RAs are generally well-understood, but the application in Alzheimer’s and addiction trials has revealed distinct clinical concerns.Gastrointestinal and General TolerabilityIn the elderly populations targeted in AD trials, gastrointestinal (GI) side effects remain the primary challenge to adherence. In the ELAD trial, $25.5\%$ of all adverse events in the liraglutide group were GI-related, primarily nausea and vomiting. Despite this, the rate of serious adverse events (SAEs) was unexpectedly lower in the active treatment group ($6.9\%$) compared to the placebo arm ($17.6\%$). This phenomenon is likely due to the drug’s beneficial effects on overall cardiovascular and metabolic health, which may reduce the frequency of other common illnesses in the elderly.In the addiction trials, which typically involve younger and more metabolically diverse populations, the safety profile has been similarly favorable.
