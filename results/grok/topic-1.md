**Query:** Analyze Phase 2 and Phase 3 clinical trial data from 2023 to present evaluating GLP-1 receptor agonists for two specific non-weight-loss indications: Alzheimer's disease and alcohol/substance addiction. For each indication, contrast the actual peer-reviewed efficacy data and reported adverse event rates against mainstream media narratives and corporate pipeline projections. Identify where the evidence is strong, where it is preliminary, and where media coverage has outpaced the science.

---

## Alzheimer's Disease (AD)

Phase 2 and 3 data from 2023 onward for GLP-1 receptor agonists (primarily liraglutide and semaglutide; limited or absent dedicated trials for tirzepatide or dulaglutide beyond observational) show **no robust clinical efficacy** for slowing symptomatic AD progression, with only preliminary or exploratory neuroprotective signals.

The key Phase 2b trial (ELAD, NCT01843075; liraglutide 1.8 mg daily injection vs placebo, n=204 participants with mild-to-moderate AD, 52 weeks, published *Nature Medicine* 2025/2026) missed its primary endpoint of change in cerebral glucose metabolic rate on FDG-PET (adjusted difference −0.17, 95% CI −0.39 to 0.06, P=0.14). Secondary cognition showed a modest benefit on the ADAS-Executive domain z-score (difference +0.15, 95% CI 0.03–0.28, unadjusted P=0.01; roughly 18% slower decline in completers per presentations). No significant effects on ADCS-ADL or CDR-Sum of Boxes. Exploratory MRI brain volumetry indicated slower atrophy (e.g., temporal lobe +696 mm³ less loss, 95% CI 184–1,208, P<0.001; total gray matter +7,274 mm³, P=0.002; similar for parietal/frontal regions). Earlier smaller exenatide Phase 2 trials showed mixed or null cognitive/biomarker effects. Systematic reviews up to 2024 generally found no consistent cognition or core AD pathology (amyloid/tau) improvements.

The landmark Phase 3 trials (EVOKE and EVOKE+, oral semaglutide 14 mg flexible dose vs placebo, n≈3,808 with early symptomatic AD/MCI, ≈104 weeks; Novo Nordisk) definitively failed the primary endpoint of change in CDR-Sum of Boxes (differences ≈−0.08 to +0.10 points across trials, non-significant P>0.4; changes ≈2.2–2.3 points worsening in both arms). All main secondary clinical outcomes (cognition, function, progression) were negative. Some CSF/plasma biomarkers improved modestly (p-tau reductions up to ≈10%, neurogranin/YKL-40, inflammation markers like hsCRP). Full results appeared in *The Lancet* (2026). No other completed large Phase 3 readouts exist for any GLP-1 in AD.

### Adverse Events (AD)

Adverse event rates were consistent with the GLP-1 class and generally tolerable. In ELAD (liraglutide), gastrointestinal events accounted for 25.5% of all reported AEs (higher incidence vs placebo: nausea, diarrhea, anorexia, bloating); weight loss was more common (≥5% in 39.2% vs 12.6% placebo; 5–10% in 8.9% vs 1.1%). Total AEs were similar by participant count, but serious AEs were lower in the liraglutide arm (6.9% vs 17.6% of participants; most unrelated). No excess discontinuations or new safety signals in non-diabetic AD patients; profile described as clinically acceptable. In EVOKE/EVOKE+ (semaglutide), treatment-emergent AEs occurred in 91% vs 85% placebo; primarily GI (nausea ≈24%, diarrhea/vomiting ≈12–14%, appetite decrease ≈33%); weight loss ≈5.8–6% vs minimal gain in placebo. Serious/severe/fatal AEs were similar or lower; discontinuations and dose reductions higher due to GI effects (≈17% vs 9%), but overall safety aligned with prior semaglutide experience (millions of patient-years). No new neuropsychiatric risks or excess falls/injuries beyond known class effects.

### Media vs. Science (AD)

Mainstream media narratives heavily emphasized potential "breakthrough" or "disease-modifying" effects pre-2025, often framing GLP-1s (especially semaglutide/Ozempic) as brain-protective or transformative based on preclinical neuroprotection, observational dementia-risk reduction in T2D cohorts (HRs ≈0.6–0.9 vs other antidiabetics), and early liraglutide imaging signals. Headlines and stories invoked "Ozempic for Alzheimer's" or "wonder drug" potential. Post-November 2025 EVOKE failure, coverage shifted to "disappointing," "fails to slow progression," or tempered notes on biomarkers/prevention possibilities, with some acknowledging overhype.

### Corporate Pipeline (AD)

Corporate pipeline projections (Novo Nordisk primarily; Eli Lilly secondary) positioned semaglutide as a high-upside repurposing opportunity despite "low likelihood of success," with large, rigorous Phase 3 investment framed as responsible exploration of metabolic/neuroprotective mechanisms. Post-failure, Novo discontinued the 1-year extensions, highlighted biomarker improvements and overall semaglutide safety/efficacy in approved indications, and did not advance further dedicated AD trials. Liraglutide received less corporate push. No major tirzepatide AD programs. Observational dementia-risk data fueled optimism, but clinical translation failed.

### Assessment (AD)

Evidence is **strong** for safety and **lack of clinical disease modification** in symptomatic early/mild-moderate AD (large Phase 3 negative on primary/secondary clinical endpoints). **Preliminary** for modest neuroprotective signals (liraglutide Phase 2 secondary/exploratory cognition and brain-volume preservation; biomarkers across trials; observational prevention hints in T2D). **Media and some pre-trial corporate narratives significantly outpaced the science** on treatment efficacy claims; the Phase 3 failure highlighted that biomarker or mechanistic promise did not translate to clinical slowing. Potential remains speculative for prevention or adjunctive use in comorbid populations, but dedicated indications lack support.

## Alcohol/Substance Use Disorder (AUD/SUD)

Data here are earlier-stage: no completed positive Phase 3 efficacy trials for any GLP-1 as primary SUD treatment (ongoing/recruiting Phase 2/3, including Lilly's dual GLP-1/GIP agonist brenipatide programs). Evidence rests on one small Phase 2 RCT, earlier mixed Phase 2s, and strong consistent observational/real-world associations.

The key Phase 2 RCT (semaglutide once-weekly subcutaneous, low-dose titration to 0.5–1 mg vs placebo, n=48 non-treatment-seeking adults with AUD, 9 weeks outpatient + lab self-administration; *JAMA Psychiatry* 2025) showed significant reductions vs placebo on primary lab outcomes (grams alcohol consumed β=−0.48, 95% CI −0.85 to −0.11, P=0.01; peak breath alcohol concentration β=−0.46, P=0.03; medium-to-large effects). Secondary real-world drinking: drinks per drinking day reduced (β=−0.41, P=0.04); heavy drinking days trajectory improved (interaction β=0.84, P=0.04); weekly craving reduced (Penn Alcohol Craving Scale β=−0.39, P=0.01). No significant effects on average drinks per day or drinking vs abstinent days. Subgroup cigarette reduction in current smokers. Effects dose-dependent and larger at higher exposure. Earlier exenatide Phase 2 (n=127, 26 weeks) was negative on primary heavy drinking days overall but showed fMRI reward-cue reactivity reduction and benefit in obese subgroup; dulaglutide secondary analysis (smoking cessation RCT) showed ≈29% lower weekly alcohol intake. No dedicated large Phase 3 readouts yet; several Phase 2 (semaglutide/tirzepatide) and emerging Phase 3 (dual agonists) are underway.

### Observational Data (AUD/SUD)

Observational data are robust and consistent across large cohorts. A major VA emulation target-trial analysis (n>600k T2D veterans initiating GLP-1 RA vs SGLT2 inhibitors; *BMJ* 2026) found lower incident SUD risk (composite HR 0.86, 95% CI 0.83–0.88; alcohol HR 0.82; opioids 0.75; nicotine/cocaine/cannabis ≈0.80–0.86; number-needed-to-treat benefits −5.6 to −1 per 1,000). In those with pre-existing SUD, reduced adverse outcomes (ED visits HR 0.69; hospitalizations 0.74; mortality 0.50; overdose 0.61). Consistent across semaglutide/liraglutide/dulaglutide, subgroups, and sensitivity analyses. Other EHR studies (e.g., semaglutide vs other anti-obesity or antidiabetic meds) reported 44–56% lower AUD incidence/recurrence over 1–3 years. Real-world reports also note reduced healthcare events and cross-substance effects.

### Adverse Events (AUD/SUD)

Adverse events mirrored the class and were mild/tolerable in AUD populations. In the semaglutide Phase 2: primarily expected GI/appetite effects (no serious AEs, no treatment-related discontinuations, no alcohol interactions); ≈5% body-weight loss (advantageous contextually). No worsening of depression, blood pressure, or glycemic issues. Observational and other trials report similar GI predominance without excess severe events or SUD-specific safety concerns.

### Media vs. Science (AUD/SUD)

Mainstream media narratives amplified patient anecdotes and early signals into broad claims of "Ozempic kills cravings," "cures addiction," or transformative SUD treatment (alcohol, opioids, nicotine, etc.), often citing real-world weight-loss patients reporting diminished substance desire. Coverage surged 2023–2026 with headlines framing GLP-1s as unexpected "anti-addiction" drugs, sometimes extrapolating from observational or preclinical reward-pathway data. Post-small RCT, stories remained optimistic ("promising," "game-changer potential") with less tempering than in AD.

### Corporate Pipeline (AUD/SUD)

Corporate pipeline projections are more exploratory than for AD. Novo Nordisk has limited dedicated SUD programs (some Phase 2 or secondary analyses; post-AD setback, focus remains metabolic). Eli Lilly is more active, with Phase 3 brenipatide (dual agonist) trials in AUD (and Phase 2 in opioid/nicotine relapse prevention) expected to read out 2027–2028; tirzepatide also under investigation. Emphasis is on mechanistic overlap with reward/metabolic pathways rather than core pipeline priority. Projections highlight broad SUD preventive/harm-reduction potential but tie it to ongoing trials rather than imminent approvals.

### Assessment (AUD/SUD)

Evidence is **strong** for observational associations (consistent risk reduction and harm mitigation across alcohol and multiple substances in large real-world data). **Preliminary** for causal efficacy (small Phase 2 signals on craving, consumption intensity, and lab intake; larger confirmatory RCTs/Phase 3 pending). Safety is well-established with mild GI effects. **Media narratives and anecdotal amplification have outpaced rigorous controlled data** (small n, short duration, non-treatment-seeking samples in the main RCT; "cure" framing exceeds evidence), though directionally aligned with emerging signals and observational strength—unlike AD, where Phase 3 was clearly negative. Potential appears strongest as adjunctive therapy in patients with comorbid obesity/T2D/SUD, but dedicated SUD indications require completed larger trials for regulatory or guideline support. Brain-penetration differences among agonists (e.g., higher for dulaglutide in some preclinical measures) may influence future optimization.

---

## Overall Summary

In both indications, GLP-1 receptor agonists demonstrate a consistent, tolerable safety profile dominated by transient gastrointestinal effects and weight loss (often beneficial or neutral here). Core AD pathology or SUD consumption reduction claims have not yet translated to strong clinical endpoints in dedicated Phase 3 programs. Media enthusiasm—fueled by mechanistic plausibility, observational data, and patient stories—has frequently run ahead of peer-reviewed efficacy results, particularly for disease modification in AD and broad "cure" potential in addiction. Corporate efforts reflect cautious optimism with targeted investment (large but ultimately negative in AD; exploratory and ramping in SUD). The science remains strongest around safety, biomarkers/volume preservation (AD), and real-world risk reduction (both), while efficacy for primary non-weight-loss indications stays preliminary or unproven pending further Phase 3 outcomes.
