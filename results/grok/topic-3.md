---

Northern Virginia (primarily Loudoun and Prince William counties, aka "Data Center Alley") hosts the world's largest concentration of hyperscale data centers—roughly one-third of global capacity—with over 200 facilities and significant additional pipeline. Growth accelerated post-2022 amid the AI boom, but grid constraints, transmission bottlenecks, and local zoning backlash have created major headwinds since at least 2022.

## Energy Grid Constraints and PJM Data

PJM Interconnection (the regional transmission organization covering NoVA) and Dominion Energy Virginia have repeatedly flagged unprecedented load growth. A 2022 PJM memo warned of transmission violations and "pinch points" in eastern Loudoun County due to data center-driven demand starting ~2018, with new connections potentially delayed to 2026.

Dominion's February 2026 State Corporation Commission (SCC) filing (related to PUR-2026-00011 and large-load queue processes) disclosed ~70,000 MW (70 GW) of advancing large-load data requests—nearly triple the Dominion Zone's all-time peak of 24,678 MW (January 23, 2025). New requests average 2–3 GW per month.

Dominion's September 2025 PJM submission projected data center billing demand at 4.2 GW for 2025 (up from ~3.6 GW in 2024), rising to ~5.9 GW by 2028 and ~7 GW by 2030. About 30 GW+ of engineering letters of authorization (ELOAs) were under contract as of mid-2025; 7 customers account for 72% of demand. A temporary Loudoun transmission constraint is slated for relief in 2026 via new infrastructure.

PJM's broader picture includes a ~6.6 GW capacity shortfall in the December 2025 auction (first failure in history), record-high/capped prices in 2025–2027 auctions driven largely by data center load, and interconnection queue backlogs (reformed from 140+ GW to ~46 GW). Data centers are projected to drive a substantial portion of PJM's ~32 GW peak load growth through 2030 in the Dominion zone. PJM's 2025/2026 Regional Transmission Expansion Plan (RTEP) approved ~$11.8 billion in upgrades (including ~$4.8 billion Dominion-led), with new 500 kV lines and projects (e.g., potential HVDC backbone for ~3 GW to Loudoun by ~2032) phased in 2026–2032.

In January 2026, the U.S. Department of Energy authorized PJM to direct NoVA data centers to run emergency diesel generators during extreme cold, highlighting reliability strain.

## Utility Filings and Regulatory Responses

Dominion SCC filings: Include updated Integrated Resource Plans (IRPs) citing data centers as the primary load driver, a 2026 large-load queue process, and transmission applications (e.g., Golden-Mars 500 kV line in Loudoun, ~$402 million, filed 2025).

SCC November 2025 biennial review order: Created a new GS-5 rate class for customers ≥25 MW (primarily data centers), effective January 2027. Requires minimum payments (85% of contracted transmission/distribution demand, 60% generation) via 14-year contracts to shield residential ratepayers. Approved residential rate hikes (~7.5% or ~$16/month initially) but directed further cost-allocation reviews.

2026 Virginia legislation (some passed): Allows utilities to delay service to >90 MW loads for reliability; shifts certain auction/distribution costs to large users (with SCC approval). Many broader reform bills stalled.

## Zoning and Local Government Decisions

**Loudoun County** (epicenter): Data centers were long "by-right" in some zones, but resident backlash over noise, visual impacts, land use, and grid strain led to changes. In March 2025, the Board of Supervisors eliminated by-right zoning; all new proposals now require Special Exception (SPEX) approval with public hearings before the Planning Commission and Board. Grandfathering applies narrowly to pre-February 2025 applications (with conditions). Phase 1 (2024–2025) removed by-right use; Phase 2 (approved September 2025) focuses on updated standards for compatibility, noise, height, substations, energy storage, and siting without a full moratorium (prohibited by state law). Some applications have been scaled or denied.

**Prince William County:** Similar pushback. The county updated noise ordinances (2023) and formed a Data Center Ordinance Advisory Group. A major rezoning for the ~23 million sq ft "Digital Gateway" project (rural/agricultural land near Manassas National Battlefield) was voided ab initio by circuit court (2025) and upheld by the Virginia Court of Appeals (March 31/April 2026) due to improper public notice for 2023 hearings. This halts the project for now (county/developers may appeal further). Broader efforts include bills to restrict data centers to industrial zones and setbacks from residential areas.

Overall, local governments face fiscal dependence (data centers generate massive tax revenue, enabling residential tax cuts in Loudoun) but growing opposition over infrastructure costs, environment, and quality of life. State law limits outright moratoria.

## Comparison to Emerging Secondary Markets (Ohio, Texas, Nordics)

These markets compete aggressively for AI/hyperscale facilities, offering stronger grid readiness, cheaper/faster power, and (in some cases) incentives—driving diversification away from constrained NoVA.

**Ohio** (PJM territory, Columbus emerging hub): Strong incentives (~$2.5 billion total since 2017, including ~$140–150 million/year sales tax exemption on equipment; property abatements; job-creation credits). However, pushback includes reports warning of ~$70/month residential bill hikes by 2028 if unchecked, and calls to nix breaks or require self-generation. Grid: AEP and partners advancing ~10 GW gas generation + $4.2 billion transmission (developer-funded in some cases) for data centers; PUCO approved tariffs requiring large loads to pay minimum 85% of contracted capacity (up to 12 years) and viability/exit fees, lifting some moratoriums.

**Texas** (ERCOT territory): Explosive growth with minimal regulation, cheap land/gas, no state income tax, and tax abatements/exemptions on equipment/power. ERCOT's deregulated market enables direct PPAs, behind-the-meter generation, and co-location. SB6 (2025) mandates large loads (>75 MW) bear interconnection/upgrade costs, provide security, and allow curtailment. Queue: Hundreds of GW requests (many speculative); 6.5 GW under construction; projections of ~24 GW data center demand by 2031 and potential to overtake NoVA as world's largest market by 2030 (JLL). Abundant renewables/gas for on-site power; 64% of new North American capacity in "frontier" markets like this.

**Nordics** (Sweden, Norway, Finland, Iceland): Natural advantages dominate—abundant cheap renewables (hydro/wind/nuclear; 90–98% low-carbon, prices often ~$0.03/kWh), cool climate (free cooling, low PUE), stable grids, and connectivity. Hyperscalers (Microsoft $3.2B+ in Sweden, Google €1B+ in Finland, OpenAI, CoreWeave) investing billions; rapid growth. Sustainable appeal for "green AI"; less local opposition/density issues than NoVA.

## Realistic Outlook for New AI Data Center Capacity Through 2028

U.S. data center load/capacity is projected to roughly double (~80 GW in 2025 to ~150 GW by 2028, or hundreds of TWh electricity demand), driven by AI (power density far higher than traditional cloud).

**NoVA:** Absolute growth continues (pipeline deliveries, post-2026 transmission relief, ~5–6+ GW additional DC demand by 2028 per Dominion forecasts), but moderated/slower than prior boom. Grid delays, zoning requirements, court blocks, local opposition, and cost pressures will limit new greenfield hyperscale campuses. Market share expected to decline sharply (~35% drop per analyses) as hyperscalers diversify.

**Overall U.S./Global:** Tens of GW new AI-focused capacity realistic. Diversification to secondary markets (Texas leading surge, Ohio via dedicated power, Nordics for green compute) accelerates; co-location with generation, PPAs, behind-the-meter plants, SMRs (longer-term), and grid-enhancing tech will be key. Risks include persistent interconnection/transmission delays, supply chain/permitting bottlenecks, higher costs passed to ratepayers, and reliability events.

**In short, AI data center expansion is not stopping—it is relocating and adapting.** NoVA will add meaningful capacity through 2028 but faces structural limits; emerging markets are better positioned for the next wave of hyperscale growth. Power availability and regulatory certainty remain the decisive factors.
